Diamond History

Origin of Diamonds

Research results by Australian scientists show that diamonds in the world are formed from organic substances deep in the ancient seabed.> History of gold mine formation


A group of scientists led by Professor Lynton Jaques, Australian National University, published their work in the famous scientific journal Geology. "The research really ends the long-standing debate about how diamonds are formed," said Mr.

The group of scientists conducted research on the most beautiful diamond samples from across Western Australia, Botswana and Venezuela... As a result, they found that the above samples had surprisingly similar structures and chemical compositions. This is because they were all formed deep inside the earth.

"Diamonds originate from organic substances lying on the ancient seabed. Then, under the influence of subduction, those substances were brought deep into the earth's crust to form beautiful diamonds," said Mr. Jaques.

"Our recent research results have proven this. It is also the only way to explain why the structure and chemical composition of diamonds are completely different from all other materials on the Earth's surface." Subduction is the process by which two or more continental plates of the Earth are pulled together. Then one plate moves under another and sinks into the Earth. After diamonds are formed here, they will be brought to the surface by geological movements, more specifically volcanic activity. That is the reason why the most beautiful diamonds are often found in extinct volcanoes, deep in the Earth's interior.

Exploitation and Market

About 49% of diamonds are mined in Central and South Africa, although significant quantities are also found in Canada, India, Russia, Brazil, and Australia. Most are mined in extinct volcanoes, deep within the Earth where high pressure and temperature change the structure of the crystals. Diamond mining has also been the subject of controversy. There has also been some controversy that the De Beers group has used its monopoly on the diamond supply to manipulate market prices, although the company's market share has fallen to 50% in recent years.

Only about 20% of the world's diamond production is used for jewellery, with 80% of lower quality diamonds used for industrial and research applications.
